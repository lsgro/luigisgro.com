Value proposition

Developing a software application means building new ways for humans to interact productively with a computer.

Like a specialised language, an application enables two-ways communication with a counterparty — the computing machine — for a specific goal.

When the machine is driven by the user to perform a task, it relays back some notion of its inner workings.

The fidelity of this feedback loop is essential to the quality of user experience: as with any sophisticated instrument, by using a computer we iteratively learn what it enables us to do, and we adjust, in response to that, the realm of our creative possibilities.

Arbitrary limitations, poor performance, lack of self-consistency, rather than being simply annoying traits of software applications, which make our work more difficult than it needs to be, are in fact hampering our ability to reason in innovative ways: like an out-of-tune piano, a poorly developed application affects creativity at its source, impeding an ideal user performance with its own idiosyncrasies and impossibilities.

Developing applications in a way that enables an empowering level of interaction is only possible by carefully and professionally mediating between the client's applicative domain and a properly selected stack of technologies.

Minimum viable products, early and frequent releases are vital practices for success, but they must be accompanied by a speculative activity, continuously challenging the current domain model, ready to revise and rework architecture and code when hypotheses are falsified.

Technical skills must not be a barrier to erect as a protection from the world, but the means for a developer to display and let others experiment with a novel view of the world: a living thing that is destined to be tested, criticised, recast, amended, redone, improved perpetually, precisely because there is value in it.

Past projects

Risk analytics for equity trading

Working as a freelance for a small team of portfolio managers at a leading hedge fund, I have developed a custom risk analytics system.

To speed-up the development and enable the PM team to maintain the application by themselves, I chose to write it in Python, leveraging Pandas and other scientific libraries. The application, deployed on Docker containers on company hardware, was available both as a dependency in a centralised Jupyter setup, and as a set of REST API, which have been integrated in the company trader workbench by another developer.

After being successfully used by the original team, the application was selected to be distributed to all equity trading portfolio managers in the hedge fund. I contributed to this effort in another assignment by adding support for more financial instruments and integrating an additional pricing library.

Tech stack: Python, Pandas, Jupyter, Docker, Postgres, REST, Bloomberg API

Risk and pricing application for volatility trading

Working for a medium-size equity hedge fund, I was responsible for the development of a new application for risk analytics.

I wrote the backend of the application in Scala, leveraging AWS Lambda to distribute the pricing tasks. The almost real time performance achieved in evaluating the whole portfolio, which included hundreds of derivatives, gave the traders a trusted and convenient way to monitor their risk. Furthermore, the clean architecture of the application, facilitated by the choice of the language, made it possible to refine the pricing models and add new instruments quickly when needed.

Tech stack: Scala, AWS Lambda, Redis, Microsoft SQL, REST, Bloomberg API

Open-source framework for algo-trading

During an interim period dedicated to experimenting first-hand with equity trading and quantitative market analysis, I have developed a trading application written in Java as an Eclipse IDE plugin.

The application, which included charting, a rudimentary backtesting engine, market data feeds, paper and live trading capability with a broker, was released as open source: https://github.com/lsgro/quantcomponents

Tech stack: Java, Eclipse IDE, SWT

Order management system for flow bond trading

While employed by a software vendor specialised in trading applications for banks, I was responsible for re-engineering an order management application written in Java.

The new architecture I envisioned was based on a modular setup, implemented with Spring container, and was able to process multiple orders concurrently, using thread executors and concurrent queues for the various states of the order.

Tech stack: Java, Spring, Bloomberg API, Reuters API, FIX

Web application for cruise booking

Working for a medium-size cruise operator, I was given the job of building a B2B web application using the existing terminal-based booking system as backend.

As the leader of a team of two, I developed an adapter exposing an API to access the terminal-based backend using a screen-scraping technique written in Perl.

Tech stack: PHP, Perl

My (tech) story

Early 80s: Genoa, Italy

Being part of the first generation of teen-agers with access to a personal computer, I took my first steps in geekiness tinkering with an 8-bit CPU, 16KB of RAM and an arcane set of machine code instructions, to be encoded to integers on paper.

1996: Linux, the Internet

When, during my university studies, the World Wide Web became accessible to everyone with a modem, and the first usable versions of Linux became available to download on floppy disks, I committed to figuring out how all of this worked. The technology stack I was using at the time was still small enough to fit in the head of a motivated student of electronic engineering: x86 CPU, C language, Andy Tanenbaum's books, the Linux kernel source code, the UNIX shell, TCP/IP, HTTP, CGI and the early versions of Javascript. I have spent innumerable hours in study rooms, libraries, at home tinkering with my 80386 PC, perusing source code and experimenting with compilers and disassemblers.

Meanwhile I had landed my first full time job as a C/SQL programmer on UNIX./p>

2000s: my career as a software developer

Over the years I have managed to keep my priorities clear: avoiding as much as possible company politics, and rather concentrating on the craft of creating software and continuing to learn: keeping up-to-date with new technologies, liaise with local experts, mentoring junior developers, meeting with users, designing applications, reading and writing lots of code, fixing lots of bugs.

2010: London, UK

The same priorities, and an interest in quantitative finance, took me to the City of London, where investment banks were boosting the ranks of developers after the 2008 season of downsizing, and where, while working as a Java developer in Canary Wharf during the day, I was able to attend Paul Wilmott's lessons in Moorgate in the evenings and eventually achieve a Certificate in Quantitative Finance.

2013: Zurich, Switzerland

Trying to put my newly acquired skills to work, and looking to move to Switzerland, I got a new job in Zurich.

The renaissance of functional programming in the industry was in full spin, thanks to Scala, Clojure and F#. In my new job, thanks to the amazing talents I was working with, I became proficient with functional Scala, experiencing first hand how an improvement in technology can make you so much more productive and happier.

2016: Zug, Switzerland

Working as a quantitative developer in two hedge funds, I had the opportunity to consolidate my skills in scientific computing, collaborating with quants and traders, using Python and its ecosystem for financial applications.

2023: Lugano, Switzerland

After several fulfilling years and two successful green-field projects I realised that, to stay true to my priorities — continuing to learn and being creative — I should take some time off, away from all the necessary schedules and routine activities of company work; digress for a while into interesting topics (machine learning, systematic trading, Rust); try to do my part in making things more intelligible and functional, with code and words.
